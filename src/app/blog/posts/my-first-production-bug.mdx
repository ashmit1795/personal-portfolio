I'm going to be honest about this one.

During my internship at Nurdd, I introduced a bug in production. Not intentionally — I was fixing something else and didn't think through the side effects carefully enough.

The bug wasn't catastrophic. No data was lost. But a background job started failing silently for about half a day before anyone caught it.

Here's what actually happened.

---

## What happened

I was modifying an API endpoint that triggered a background workflow. The workflow ran asynchronously — it processed some incoming data and dispatched a notification to users.

I changed the shape of the payload the endpoint passed to the workflow. The endpoint worked fine. Tests passed. I merged the PR.

What I didn't check: the workflow validated its incoming payload schema. My change broke that validation. The workflow started failing immediately after being queued — but because the failure was async, nothing showed up in the API logs. The endpoint kept returning 200s. Everything looked fine.

Half a day of silent failures. Notifications never sent.

---

## What I did wrong

A few things, and they're worth naming specifically:

**I tested the endpoint, not the system.** My tests confirmed that the API returned the right response. They didn't confirm that the downstream workflow actually ran to completion.

**I didn't trace the full data flow.** I understood the endpoint. I understood the workflow existed. I didn't clearly think through the *contract* between them and whether my change violated it.

**I assumed silence meant success.** This is probably the most important lesson. When a background job fails and nobody has instrumented it properly, it fails *silently*. Silent failures are worse than loud ones because they give you false confidence that everything is working.

---

## What I'd do differently

Read the full data flow before touching anything. Not just the function I'm changing — the entire path from input to final outcome.

Write tests that cross the boundary between components, not just tests that validate each component in isolation.

Log aggressively in async code. A background job that fails without a trace is effectively invisible.

And most importantly: *when you change a thing, ask yourself what else depends on that thing.*

---

## Why I'm writing this

Not because the bug was particularly interesting. Because the lesson from it keeps showing up everywhere.

Every system has contracts between components. Sometimes they're explicit (typed interfaces, schemas). Sometimes they're implicit (shape of a payload, order of operations, timing assumptions). When you change one side of a contract without updating the other, something breaks.

The code was easy to fix. Developing the habit of thinking about contracts — that took longer.
