When I joined [Nurdd](https://nurdd.club/) as a Backend Developer Intern, I expected to write APIs, fix bugs, and contribute to backend features.

What I didn't expect was being trusted with production systems that served thousands of users, processing large volumes of creator data, designing scalable architectures, improving AI systems, and learning what ownership truly means in software engineering.

Looking back, this internship wasn't just another item on my résumé.

It fundamentally changed the way I think about building software.

---

## About Nurdd

Nurdd is an AI-powered marketing intelligence platform that helps brands make smarter creator marketing decisions.

The platform enables businesses to discover creators, analyze influencer performance, measure AI search visibility, and understand marketing insights through large-scale data processing and AI-driven workflows.

Behind every creator profile that users search lies an enormous amount of engineering.

Data has to be collected from multiple sources, enriched with analytics, processed through different services, stored efficiently, and finally served with low latency.

During my internship, I had the opportunity to work on exactly these kinds of problems.

## Learning That Production Is Very Different From College Projects

In university projects, we often think about making something work.

In production, making something work is only the beginning.

You also have to think about:

* scalability
* concurrency
* failures
* retries
* latency
* infrastructure cost
* user experience
* observability
* reliability

That mindset shift became one of the biggest lessons from my internship.

---

## Solving a Multi-Minute Request Problem

One of the first large engineering challenges I worked on involved creator profile generation.

At first glance, it sounded simple.

A user searches for a creator.

The backend returns the profile.

But behind that request, the platform needed to fetch and aggregate data from several different sources, calculate analytics, and normalize everything into a usable format.

If the creator already existed in our database, the response was almost instantaneous.

But if it was a completely new creator, all of these operations had to happen before returning a response — and that process could take several minutes.

Clearly, keeping a user waiting that long wasn't an option.

---

## Designing a More Responsive Architecture

Instead of blocking the request until all processing finished, I designed and implemented a background processing workflow that decoupled long-running operations from the user request lifecycle.

The request flow became much simpler:

1. Check whether the creator already exists.
2. If yes, return immediately.
3. Otherwise, kick off processing in the background.
4. Notify waiting users once it completes.

The frontend remained responsive while the heavy lifting happened behind the scenes.

That single architectural decision dramatically improved the user experience. I won't go into the specific implementation details here, but it's the kind of problem that taught me a lot about designing for responsiveness at scale.

---

## The Concurrency Problem Nobody Notices

One interesting challenge appeared once background processing was in place.

Imagine ten users searching for the same creator simultaneously.

Without additional safeguards, the system would happily start ten identical jobs.

That meant duplicate processing, higher infrastructure costs, and unnecessary load.

This is one of those production problems that rarely appears in classroom assignments but happens all the time in real systems.

To solve this, I implemented concurrency control mechanisms that ensured duplicate processing was avoided while still allowing multiple users to safely request the same resource. Only one job could exist for a given piece of work at any time, and everyone waiting on it received the result once it was ready.

The system became significantly more efficient while reducing duplicate work.

---

## Reliability Matters More Than Success

Another lesson I learned was that production systems must expect failure.

External services fail.

Networks fail.

Servers restart.

Instead of assuming everything would succeed, I built the processing pipeline to recover automatically — safely retrying transient failures while isolating persistent ones for later investigation.

Rather than crashing silently, failed jobs could now be inspected, debugged, and replayed.

Building for failure became just as important as building for success.

---

## Scaling Creator Data Ingestion

Another major area I worked on involved ingesting creator data from external platforms — fetching metadata, collecting analytics, enriching creator information, and handling large-scale onboarding.

The architecture again leaned on asynchronous processing rather than synchronous requests, which helped keep ingestion scalable while reducing request latency as the platform continued to grow.

## Stepping Into AI Systems

One of the most exciting opportunities during my internship came when I started working on Nurdd's AI chatbot.

Initially, the chatbot relied on multiple sequential steps for each user message — content filtering, query rewriting, context resolution, intent classification, and routing.

Although functional, every additional step increased latency, resource usage, orchestration complexity, and opportunities for error.

I wasn't originally hired to work on AI systems — most of my prior experience was elsewhere in the stack. But that's one thing I appreciated about Nurdd: good ideas were welcomed regardless of who proposed them.

I spent time learning what I needed to and eventually redesigned the chatbot into a simpler, more unified architecture that could dynamically pull in additional information only when it was actually needed, instead of working through a long fixed chain of steps.

The improvements were immediately noticeable:

* lower latency
* fewer processing steps
* better context retention
* improved conversational accuracy
* simpler orchestration
* lower resource consumption

It was one of the most rewarding engineering problems I worked on because it combined backend engineering with applied AI.

## Making Conversations More Natural

Another exciting contribution involved adding voice capabilities to the chatbot, so users could interact by speaking instead of typing.

Watching a system evolve from text-only interactions into a richer, multimodal experience was incredibly satisfying.

## Ownership Is More Than Writing Code

Perhaps the biggest lesson from this internship wasn't technical.

It was learning what ownership actually means.

Ownership meant asking:

* What happens if this service fails?
* How will this behave under load?
* What happens if two users do the same thing simultaneously?
* Can this architecture scale six months from now?
* How do we debug this in production?

I wasn't simply implementing assigned tasks.

I was expected to think through the engineering decisions behind them.

That responsibility accelerated my growth far more than simply writing more code ever could.

No engineering journey happens alone.

One of the reasons this internship became such a memorable experience was the people.

The senior developers were incredibly supportive throughout my internship.

They never hesitated to explain architectural decisions, review implementations, or challenge my assumptions in ways that helped me grow.

That culture encouraged curiosity instead of hesitation.

It's something I'll always be grateful for.

## Looking Back

When I started this internship, I thought backend engineering was mostly about APIs and databases.

Today, I see it very differently.

Backend engineering is about designing systems that remain reliable under pressure.

It's about making software resilient, scalable, observable, and maintainable.

It's about solving problems users never even realize existed because the system simply works.

Over the course of my internship, I had the opportunity to work with asynchronous processing, concurrency control, third-party integrations, production debugging, reliability engineering, AI-driven chat systems, and voice interfaces.

More importantly, I learned how experienced engineers think when building production software.

That lesson will stay with me far longer than any individual technology.

To everyone at Nurdd—thank you for trusting me with real engineering problems, encouraging my ideas, and creating an environment where learning never stopped.

This internship wasn't just where I wrote code.

It was where I started thinking like a software engineer.
